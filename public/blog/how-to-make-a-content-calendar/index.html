How to make a content calendar for social media (step by step)

A content calendar turns "what do I post today?" into a solved problem. Here's a simple way to build one you'll actually stick to — and a free generator that does the first draft for you.

A content calendar is just a plan of what you'll post, where, and when. That's it. The reason most founders don't have one isn't complexity — it's that building it from scratch feels like a project. So let's make it a 20-minute task you do once and reuse.

Step 1 — Pick your platforms (fewer than you think)

Two done well beats five done badly. Choose the one or two places your customers actually hang out — for most B2B founders that's LinkedIn and X; for visual or local businesses, Instagram. Ignore the rest until these are humming.

Step 2 — Set a realistic cadence

Three to five posts a week is the sweet spot: frequent enough to stay top-of-mind, sparse enough to sustain. Don't promise yourself "daily" and quit by Thursday. Pick a number you can hit on your worst week.

Step 3 — Choose 4–6 repeatable angles

This is the part that kills the blank page. Instead of inventing original ideas every day, rotate a small set of angles:

  • How-to — teach one small thing.
  • Hot take — a contrarian opinion you can defend.
  • Story — a before/after from you or a customer.
  • Quick tip — a 30-second win.
  • Behind the scenes — how the work actually happens.
  • Proof — a number or result.

Five slots a week, filled from six angles, is a complete calendar — and you'll never stare at a blank page again.

Step 4 — Fill the grid

Map angles to days and platforms in a simple table (a spreadsheet is fine): Day → Platform → Angle → the actual idea. The goal is a skeleton you can write from, not a finished script.

Shortcut: our free content calendar generator does steps 3 and 4 for you. Type what your business does, pick your platforms, and it returns a full week of angle-based ideas you can copy or download as a CSV. Use it as your starting skeleton, then tweak.

Step 5 — Batch, schedule, and protect the system

Write a week in one sitting rather than scrambling daily, schedule it so publishing isn't a decision, and use guardrails instead of streaks — on a bad week, drop to two posts, never to zero. Re-entry from "two" is easy; re-entry from "zero" is where calendars die.
